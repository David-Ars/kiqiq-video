# The Sub-Elite Video Analysis Stack: What a Semi-Pro Club Can Run for Under £1,000 a Year

A National League or step-three side typically has one part-time analyst, no dedicated tracking budget, and a board that wants a build-vs-buy answer in a single page. The honest answer is that the gap between "no stack" and "elite stack" contains a workable middle tier costing roughly £600 to £900 a year. The decision is what to put in it.

## The Four Layers of an Analysis Stack

Every stack splits into four layers. Most clubs over-invest in one and ignore the other three.

**Capture layer.** The hardware that records the match. At sub-elite level this is a single mid-mast camera or a smartphone on a tactical tripod. A dual-mast setup is overkill until the analyst has someone reviewing the second angle.

**Tagging layer.** The software that lets the analyst code events, transitions, and set pieces against the timeline. This is where most of the value sits and where most clubs underspend.

**Storage and sharing layer.** Where the clipped footage lives and how it gets to coaches and players. A shared cloud folder solves 80% of the problem; a club app solves the last 20%.

**Data layer.** Optional. Event data subscriptions, tracking integration, scouting databases. Most sub-elite clubs do not need this until the head of analysis can name a specific decision the data will inform.

## The £900 Allocation

A defensible allocation for a club at step 1 to step 4 of the English pyramid, or equivalent, looks like:

| Layer | Cost (annual) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Capture (camera + tripod amortised over 3 years) | £150 | One-time hardware spread across seasons |
| Tagging software | £300 to £500 | The largest single line item |
| Storage / sharing (cloud + club drive) | £100 | Standard Google Workspace or similar |
| Set-piece database (manual spreadsheet) | £0 | Analyst time, not licence cost |
| Optional: event data (basic tier) | £200 to £300 | Skip unless a specific use case exists |

The tagging layer carries the decision-weight. Spending £500 on tagging and £0 on event data is a better stack than spending £0 on tagging and £500 on event data, because tagging produces clip packages a coach can use on Tuesday. Event data alone produces dashboards nobody opens.

## What to Buy in the Tagging Layer

The buyer's checklist for sub-elite tagging software is short. The platform must support:

- Custom tag panels per match (not a fixed event schema).
- Frame-accurate clipping and export.
- Multi-user access for at least two coaches.
- Telestration for chalkboard-style annotations on stills.

Tools such as [Metrica Sports PlayBase](https://www.metrica-sports.com?_go=david36) cover this brief at the entry tier. The same vendor's professional tier (Nexus) adds automated tracking integration, which is the correct upgrade path once the club commits to tracking data. Buying the professional tier before the tracking commitment exists is the most common over-spend at this level.

The mistake to avoid is paying for an "all-in-one" platform whose tagging layer is a thin add-on. The tagging panel is the analyst's daily tool. If it feels clunky in the demo, it will feel worse on a Monday with eight clip packages due by Tuesday morning.

## The Build-vs-Buy Decision

Some clubs propose building their own tagging tool. The argument is usually cost, occasionally control. The defensible build-vs-buy logic at sub-elite level:

Build the spreadsheet layer (set-piece database, opposition scouting notes, training load logs). These are bespoke, low-complexity, and high-value when tailored to the coach's specific questions.

Buy the tagging layer. The engineering cost of a frame-accurate, multi-user video coding platform is higher than three years of licensing. The analyst should be tagging matches, not maintaining a video player.

Subscribe to the data layer only when a specific decision needs it. "Should we sign this striker on loan?" justifies a one-month event-data trial. "We want to see what the data says" does not.

## The First-90-Days Plan for a New Analysis Hire

A part-time analyst joining a sub-elite club in pre-season has a sequencing problem. The defensible order:

Week 1 to 2: define the three tactical questions for the season. Without these, tagging produces noise.

Week 3 to 4: build the tag panel against those questions in whichever platform the club has bought.

Week 5 to 6: run the panel on two pre-season matches. Discard tags nobody uses. Add the two tags everyone keeps requesting.

Week 7 onward: produce the two-page weekly report described in any disciplined tagging workflow. Twelve clips. Two anchor patterns. One ask of the coaching staff.

If the analyst is still asking the board for £4,000 of event data in week 8, the tagging layer is not being used hard enough.

## What This Stack Will Not Do

The £900 stack will not benchmark the squad against a league of 24 teams. It will not produce automated player ratings. It will not generate xG against opposition tendencies without manual mapping. Clubs that need those outputs should price the next tier honestly, which starts around £4,000 to £6,000 a year and assumes a full-time analyst.

The point of the sub-elite stack is not to imitate the Premier League. It is to give the head coach a structured Tuesday meeting backed by twelve clips, every week, for nine months.
